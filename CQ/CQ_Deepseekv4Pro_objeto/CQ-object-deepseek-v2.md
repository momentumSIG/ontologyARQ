## Bloque 2: Materialidad y procedencia

### CQ-OBJ-05
**Question:** What is the geological or geographic source from which the raw materials of an archaeological object were originally extracted?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Materiality Module, Spatial Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E19_Physical_Object, E53_Place, E57_Material), GeoSPARQL
- **Source:** "Provenance Determination of Archaeological Metal Objects" (Pernicka et al.) — lead isotope ratios and trace elements link objects to specific mines and quarries; Guerra (2003) — XRF identifies gold sources

### CQ-OBJ-06
**Question:** What manufacturing technique was used to produce a human-made object, and what sequence of technological steps (chaine operatoire) was followed?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Production Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E12_Production, E29_Design_or_Procedure, E22_Human-Made_Object)
- **Source:** Garcia-Rovira (csa_vol_23_2015) — the chaine operatoire captures technological process and cultural choices; Guerra (2003) — manufacturing techniques leave detectable signatures in material composition

### CQ-OBJ-07
**Question:** What elemental composition, chemical fingerprint, and isotopic ratios does an archaeological object's material exhibit, as determined by scientific laboratory analysis?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Scientific Analysis Module, Materiality Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CRMsci (S19_Encounter, S21_Measurement), CIDOC CRM (E57_Material)
- **Source:** Guerra (2003) — XRF elemental composition of gold objects; Pernicka et al. — lead isotope ratios (206Pb, 207Pb, 208Pb vs 204Pb) are the standard for metal provenance

### CQ-OBJ-08
**Question:** Did the raw materials of an archaeological object travel from their geological source to a different location for manufacturing, and did the finished object subsequently move to yet another location for use or deposition?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Materiality Module, Spatial Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E19_Physical_Object, E53_Place, E9_Move), GeoSPARQL
- **Source:** Gill ("Returning Archaeological Objects to Italy") — objects travel through trade, gift exchange, looting, and auction; Guerra (2003) — provenance chain from raw material to finished object

## Bloque 3: Ciclo de vida del objeto

### CQ-OBJ-09
**Question:** What is the complete physical lifecycle sequence of an archaeological object — from raw material extraction through manufacturing, use, possible reuse/repair, circulation, deposition, post-depositional alteration, recovery, conservation, analysis, and curation?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Object Biography Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E5_Event, E12_Production, E7_Activity, E9_Move, E81_Transformation), CRMarchaeo (A4_Stratigraphic_Genesis, A1_Excavation_Process_Unit), CRMsci (S19_Encounter)
- **Source:** Gosden ("What do objects want?") — objects have lifecycles with form, genealogy, source, and effect; BF03376602 — object lifespan spans manufacture through discard

### CQ-OBJ-10
**Question:** What is the temporal difference between the known manufacturing date range of the object type and the estimated use period of this specific object instance?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Temporal Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E52_Time-Span, E12_Production, E7_Activity), OWL-Time
- **Source:** BF03376602 — "we should not equate the manufacturing range for an object type with the use range for a particular object"; ceramics can remain in use 15+ years after type ceases production

### CQ-OBJ-11
**Question:** Was an archaeological object reused or repurposed during its active life for a function substantially different from its original intended use?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Object Biography Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E7_Activity, E81_Transformation, E19_Physical_Object, E55_Type)
- **Source:** "Things in the Eye of the Beholder" — objects accumulate multiple meanings through cycles of use and reuse; Kopytoff's "cultural biography of things"

### CQ-OBJ-12
**Question:** Has an archaeological object been physically repaired or deliberately modified after its initial production, and what trace evidence of this intervention remains visible?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Object Biography Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E81_Transformation, E19_Physical_Object, E7_Activity)
- **Source:** ICCROM ("Sharing Conservation Decisions") — repair is a deliberate intervention; distinct from conservation in that repair occurred during the object's active use-life rather than post-recovery

### CQ-OBJ-13
**Question:** Through which geographic locations, cultural zones, exchange networks, or trade routes did an archaeological object circulate during its use-life?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Spatial Module, Object Biography Module

## Bloque 4: Tipología, función y significado

### CQ-OBJ-14
**Question:** Under which typological scheme and by what diagnostic criteria (morphological, technological, stylistic, functional) is an archaeological object classified, and who made that classification?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E17_Type_Assignment, E55_Type, E19_Physical_Object), SKOS, Getty AAT, ARIADNE AO-Cat
- **Source:** Rowe (1959) — typology is the foundation of archaeological classification; classification is always an assignment (E17_Type_Assignment), not an intrinsic property

### CQ-OBJ-15
**Question:** What functional role or roles has an archaeological object served during its use-life, and have these functions changed over time through functional transformation?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Object Biography Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E55_Type, E7_Activity, E81_Transformation), E17_Type_Assignment
- **Source:** Gosden ("What do objects want?") — objects are classified by what they do; functional role is not fixed but evolves through the object's lifecycle

### CQ-OBJ-16
**Question:** What cultural, symbolic, or ritual significance is attributed to an archaeological object beyond its utilitarian function?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Interpretation Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E19_Physical_Object, E55_Type), CRMinf (I4_Proposition_Set)
- **Source:** Pollard (2014) — tabua (Fijian whale teeth) as objects whose value derives from meaning, not material; Holtorf ("On Pastness") — cultural significance is distinct from functional role or material value

### CQ-OBJ-17
**Question:** Does an archaeological object exhibit the culturally perceived quality of pastness or age-value, independently of its measured chronological age?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Interpretation Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E19_Physical_Object), CRMinf (I4_Proposition_Set)
- **Source:** Holtorf ("On Pastness") — "What they have taken away is the age of the paint"; pastness is an aesthetic and cognitive experience, not a measurable physical property; Riegl's age-value: "what has been willed becomes, without intention"

### CQ-OBJ-18
**Question:** What human actors, social groups, cultural communities, or craft traditions are associated with the making, use, or cultural meaning of an archaeological object?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Object Biography Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E39_Actor, E74_Group, E12_Production, E7_Activity), CRMarchaeo (A1_Excavation_Process_Unit)
- **Source:** Gosden ("What do objects want?") — objects and humans are mutually constituted; "Things in the Eye of the Beholder" — biographies are told by specific humans, not universally true

### CQ-OBJ-30
**Question:** How did the form, materiality, or embedded cultural meaning of an archaeological object shape, enable, or constrain human behavior, practice, or social relations during its use-life?
- **Ontology modules required:** Archaeological Object Module, Object Biography Module, Interpretation Module
- **Possible ontology reuse:** CIDOC CRM (E19_Physical_Object, E7_Activity, E39_Actor), CRMinf (I4_Proposition_Set)
- **Source:** Gosden ("What do objects want?") — objects have agency: their form, materiality, and cultural meanings shape what humans can and cannot do with them; objects are not passive recipients of human action but active participants in social life; Pollard (2014) — tabua whale teeth shaped Fijian social relations through their material properties and cultural significance
